Methodology and sources

The OECD Municipal Atlas is based on both official statistics collected wihtin each country, as well as on unconventional sources acquired from diverse geospatial data sources, including satellite imagery. This page provides a detailed summary of the data sources and methodologies used to build this atlas.
Geographical definitions
OECD subnational regions are classified into two scales: large regions, referred to as Territorial Level 2 (TL2), and small regions, referred to as Territorial Level 3 (TL3). Large regions generally refer to the first government level after the national or federal one. Small regions are contained within large regions and, in the case of European countries, correspond to the NUTS3 nomenclature. For more details, check here.
OECD metropolitan areas are defined based on Functional Urban Areas (FUA). Developed in collaboration with the European Union and endorsed at the 2020 Statistical Commission of the United Nations, FUAs consist of densely populated cities together with their surrounding commuting zones. This definition looks at the full extent of cities’ labour markets to capture the economic boundaries of cities. FUAs are generally the aggregation of local units. For more details, check here.

Demography
The demographic indicators were collected from National Statistical Offices and include total population, population by age group and gender, number of births and deaths, internal and international inflows and outflows. The data presented on the maps for total population, share of population by age group, sex ratio, birth and death rates foreign-born population correspond to the latest year of data available in each country. As for population change, natural change, and net migration, the indicators presented on the maps correspond to the change over the last 5 years of data available.

Sex ratio
Sex ratio = male population / female population
Source

Population change
Population change is defined for the last 5 years of data available as follows:
Population change = Final population - Initial population = Natural change + Net migration
Source
National Statistical Offices
Natural change
Indicators related to natural change are defined for the last 5 years of data available as follows:
  • Natural change = Number of births - Number of deaths
  • Natural change rate = (Number of births - Number of deaths) / (Initial population)
  • Birth rate = Number of births / Population
  • Death rate = Number of deaths / Population
Source
National Statistical Offices
Migration
Indicators related to flows are defined for the last 5 years of data available as follows:
  • Net internal migration = Internal mobility inflows - outflows
  • Net internal migration rate = (Internal mobility inflows - outflows) / (Initial population)
  • Net international migration = International migration inflows - outflows
  • Net migration = Net internal migration + Net international migration
Source
National Statistical Offices

Energy
Renewable potential
The wind power potential is assessed by the mean wind power density, which serves as a quantitative measure of wind energy available at a specific location. It is expressed in W/m².
The solar power potential is assessed by the photovoltaic power potential, which serves as a quantitative measure of solar energy available at a specific location. It represents the amount of power generated per unit of the installed PV capacity over the long-term, and it is measured in kilowatthours per installed kilowatt-peak of the system capacity (kWh/kWp).

Cooling Degree Days
A Cooling Degree Days (CDD) is a measure of how hot the temperature was during a period of days, which serves to quantify the demand for energy needed to cool buidlings. It is defined as the sum of the difference between the daily mean temperature and the reference temperature of 22°C for days during which the mean temperature is higher than 22°C. Cooling Degree Days are assessed based on ERA5-Land using the average annual air temperature at 2 meters height.
Source
Muñoz Sabater, J., (2019): ERA5-Land monthly averaged data from 1981 to present. Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) Climate Data Store (CDS), doi: 10.24381/cds.68d2bb30
Heating Degree Days
A Heating Degree Days (HDD) is a measure of how cold the temperature was during a period of days, which serves to quantify the demand for energy needed to heat buidlings. It is defined as the sum of the difference between the reference temperature of 15°C daily and the mean temperature for days during which the mean temperature is lower than 15°C. Heating Degree Days are assessed based on ERA5-Land using the average annual air temperature at 2 meters height.
Source
Muñoz Sabater, J., (2019): ERA5-Land monthly averaged data from 1981 to present. Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) Climate Data Store (CDS), doi: 10.24381/cds.68d2bb30
Climate
Temperature trends
Temperature trends are assessed based on ERA5-Land using the average annual air temperature at 2 meters height. Increase in temperature are compared to the 1981-2010 climatology.
Daytime and nighttime land surface temperatures are assessed based on MODIS Aqua and Terra satellite measurements, which provide daily land surface temperature data at 1 km resolution.
Source

Heat stress
Hot days refer to days during which the daily maximum temperature is higher than 35°C. Hot days are assessed based on ERA5-Land using the air temperature at 2 meters height.
Tropical nights refer to nights during which the minimum temperature is higher than 20°C. Tropical nights are assessed based on ERA5-Land using the air temperature at 2 meters height.
Hot days and tropical nights are based on the air temperature and do not account for other meteorological variables impacting thermal comfort. The Universal Thermal Climate Index (UTCI) provides a more comprehensive measure of thermal comfort by considering other factors such as humidity, wind speed, and radiation, offering a more accurate assessment than air temperature alone. The UTCI also allows to assess the population exposure to different heat stress levels:
  • - 32°C < UTCI < 38°C: strong heat stress.
  • - 38°C < UTCI < 46°C: very strong heat stress.
  • - UTCI > 46°C: extreme heat stress.
These indicators were estimated using the thermal comfort indices derived from ERA5 reanalysis.
Source
Di Napoli C., Barnard C., Prudhomme C., Cloke HL and Pappenberger F. (2020): Thermal comfort indices derived from ERA5 reanalysis. Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) Climate Data Store (CDS). DOI: 10.24381/cds.553b7518 (Accessed on 01-08-2023)
Muñoz Sabater, J., (2019): ERA5-Land monthly averaged data from 1981 to present. Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S) Climate Data Store (CDS), doi: 10.24381/cds.68d2bb30
Precipitation
Total precipitation refers to the accumulated precipitation over a year and is expressed in mm.
Extreme precipitation days correspond to days during which the accumulated precipitation is higher than 20 mm.
Source

Drought
Drought is defined by the soil moisture change compared to the reference period 1981-2010. The soil moisture refers the volume of water in the first soil layer (0 to 7 cm).
